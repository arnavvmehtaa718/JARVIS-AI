# Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution began in Britain around 1760 and transformed human civilization more profoundly than any event since agriculture. Steam engines, powered by coal, replaced muscle, wind, and water, multiplying productivity and drawing millions from farms into factory cities.

Textile mills, railways, and steamships shrank the world. Iron and steel output soared, and by the late 19th century a second wave brought [[Electricity]], chemicals, and the internal combustion engine. Scientific habits inherited from the [[Renaissance]] turned invention into a systematic enterprise.

The costs were immense: brutal working conditions, child labor, and the start of large-scale fossil fuel emissions that still drive climate change today — pressures now pushing deforestation in the [[Amazon Rainforest]] and motivating the global shift to [[Renewable Energy]].

The revolution's legacy is the modern world itself: mass production, urbanization, and eventually the electronics and [[Semiconductors]] that launched the digital age, sometimes called the fourth industrial revolution and now accelerated by [[Artificial Intelligence]].
