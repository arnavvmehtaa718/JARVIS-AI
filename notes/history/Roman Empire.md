# Roman Empire

At its height around 117 AD, the Roman Empire stretched from Britain to the Persian Gulf, ruling perhaps a quarter of humanity. Its roads, aqueducts, concrete, and legal codes shaped Western civilization so deeply that Roman law still underlies many modern legal systems.

Rome absorbed and transmitted the achievements of earlier cultures, including [[Ancient Egypt]], which it annexed in 30 BC after the death of Cleopatra. Egyptian grain shipped along the [[Nile River]] and across the Mediterranean fed the city of Rome itself.

The empire's engineering was extraordinary: fourteen aqueducts supplied the capital with water, and the Colosseum could seat 50,000 spectators. Latin, its language, evolved into Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, and Romanian.

The Western empire fell in 476 AD, but the Eastern half endured as Byzantium for another thousand years. Renewed fascination with Roman art and texts later helped ignite the [[Renaissance]], proving that empires can fall yet never quite disappear.
