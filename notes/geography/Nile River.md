# Nile River

The Nile is generally regarded as the world's longest river, flowing about 6,650 kilometers north from the heart of Africa to the Mediterranean Sea. Its two main tributaries, the White Nile and the Blue Nile, meet at Khartoum before crossing the [[Sahara Desert]] in a ribbon of green.

For millennia, the river's predictable summer floods deposited rich black silt that sustained [[Ancient Egypt]], allowing dense agriculture in the middle of a desert. Egyptian civilization, from the pyramids to its temple astronomy of the [[Milky Way]], was organized around the Nile's rhythm; later the river fed the granaries of the [[Roman Empire]].

The Aswan High Dam, completed in 1970, tamed the floods and generates hydroelectric power — an early large-scale form of [[Renewable Energy]] — but also traps the silt that once renewed the fields.

Today eleven countries share the Nile basin, and disputes over dams and water rights, sharpened by climate change, make the river one of the world's key geopolitical flashpoints.
