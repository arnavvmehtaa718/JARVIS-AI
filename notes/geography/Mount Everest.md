# Mount Everest

Mount Everest, at 8,849 meters, is Earth's highest peak above sea level, straddling the border of Nepal and Tibet. It was formed — and is still rising a few millimeters each year — by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates, a slow-motion crash driven by forces as relentless as [[Gravity]] itself.

Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay made the first confirmed summit in 1953. Since then more than 6,000 people have reached the top, though the "death zone" above 8,000 meters, where oxygen is a third of sea-level pressure, has claimed over 300 lives.

The Himalayas act as Asia's water tower: their glaciers feed rivers that sustain nearly two billion people, a hydrological role comparable to the [[Nile River]] in Africa. Climate change is shrinking those glaciers rapidly, one more argument for the transition to [[Renewable Energy]].

Curiously, marine limestone near the summit means climbers stand on an ancient seafloor, older than the [[Pacific Ocean]]'s current basin.
