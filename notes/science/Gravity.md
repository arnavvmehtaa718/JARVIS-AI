# Gravity

Gravity is the force that draws objects with mass toward one another. Isaac Newton first described it mathematically in the 17th century, explaining both the fall of an apple and the orbit of [[The Moon]]. Two centuries later, Albert Einstein's general relativity reimagined gravity not as a force but as the curvature of spacetime itself.

Gravity governs the cosmos: it holds together [[The Sun]], shapes the spiral arms of the [[Milky Way]], and creates the extreme conditions inside [[Black Holes]], where its pull becomes inescapable. On Earth it drives the tides, the flow of the [[Nile River]] to the sea, and the very stability of [[Mount Everest]].

Reconciling gravity with [[Quantum Mechanics]] remains physics' greatest unsolved challenge — a theory of quantum gravity would unite the very large and the very small. In 2015, the LIGO observatories detected gravitational waves, ripples in spacetime from colliding black holes, confirming a century-old prediction of Einstein's.
