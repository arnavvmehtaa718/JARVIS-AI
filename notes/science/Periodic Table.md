# Periodic Table

The Periodic Table organizes all 118 known chemical elements by atomic number and recurring properties. Dmitri Mendeleev published his famous version in 1869 and boldly left gaps for elements not yet discovered — a gamble vindicated when they were later found.

The arrangement makes sense only through [[Quantum Mechanics]], which dictates how electrons occupy shells and thus why elements in the same column behave alike. Hydrogen and helium, the lightest elements, were forged in the Big Bang; heavier ones are cooked inside stars like [[The Sun]] and scattered by supernovae.

Silicon, a metalloid in group 14, is the backbone of [[Semiconductors]] and the entire digital age. Carbon's versatility makes it the basis of [[DNA]] and all known life. Rare earth elements, meanwhile, are critical to [[Renewable Energy]] technologies such as wind turbines and electric motors.

The table remains open-ended: laboratories continue to synthesize fleeting superheavy elements at the frontier of the known.
